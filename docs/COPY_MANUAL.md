# A1 Quality Paralegal — Copy Manual

**Purpose:** This guide governs all website copy, form language, emails, and any client-facing text. It exists to protect the business legally, build client trust, and ensure every word reflects what we actually do.

---

## 1. What We Are (and Are Not)

### The core distinction

We are a **legal document preparation service**. We are not a law firm. We do not practice law.

This distinction is not just a legal disclaimer — it is the foundation of every sentence we write.

| We do | We do not do |
|---|---|
| Prepare legal documents to the client's direction | Give legal advice |
| Provide general information about legal documents and processes | Tell clients what they should do in their specific situation |
| Ask questions that help clients clarify their own wishes | Interpret the law for a client's benefit |
| Explain what a document is and what it does | Recommend one document or strategy over another |
| File documents on behalf of clients | Represent clients in any legal proceeding |

### The "guided questions" model

We can — and should — ask pointed questions that help clients arrive at their own conclusions. This is not legal advice. The distinction is:

- **Legal advice:** "Given your situation, you should set up a revocable trust rather than a simple will."
- **Guided question:** "Do you own real property? Do you have minor children? In Oregon, an estate with real property over $200,000 and total assets over $75,000 may be subject to probate — would you like more information about what a trust does so you can decide?"

The client reaches the conclusion. We supply the information and the questions.

## 2. Voice and Tone

### Who we are

Family-owned, community-rooted, approachable. We have been doing this since 1995. We are not a tech startup and we are not a law firm. We are neighbors helping neighbors navigate paperwork that matters.

### Voice principles

**Warm but professional.** We care about our clients — genuinely. That warmth shows in our language, but we do not sacrifice clarity for friendliness.

**Plain language.** No legal jargon unless we are defining a specific document name. Write the way you would explain something to a client across a desk.

**Empowering, not directive.** Our tagline is "Helping You, Help Yourself." Copy should reinforce that the client is in control. We inform and support; we do not steer.

**Honest about our limits.** We never oversell what we do. Clarity about our role builds more trust than vague language that implies more authority than we have.

### Tone by page type

| Page | Tone |
|---|---|
| Homepage | Welcoming, reassuring, low-pressure |
| Estate Planning | Thoughtful, clear, matter-of-fact about what each document does |
| Bookings | Practical, simple, low-friction |
| Contact | Open, friendly, responsive |
| Disclaimers | Factual, neutral, non-apologetic |

## 3. Language Rules

### 3.1 Words and phrases to use

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| "prepare documents" | Accurate description of what we do |
| "document preparation" | Our legal category |
| "general information" | What we can provide |
| "to your direction" | Emphasizes client control |
| "tailored to your specifications" | Accurate, client-centered, no legal overreach |
| "tell you about the process" | Accurate and warm |
| "help you understand what each document does" | Empowering without crossing into advice |
| "some clients in similar situations..." | Shares information without prescribing |
| "you may want to consider..." | Informs without directing |
| "an attorney can advise you on..." | Transparent about referral when needed |

### 3.2 Words and phrases to avoid

| Avoid | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| "advise you" | Implies legal advice | "provide information," "tell you about the process" |
| "you should" | Prescriptive — legal advice territory | "you may want to," "some clients choose to" |
| "recommend" | Implies professional judgment on legal strategy | "explain the options," "help you compare" |
| "guidance" / "guide you" / "guided" | Loaded term — implies we are directing decisions | "walk you through," "tell you about," "help you understand," "straightforward" |
| "we will handle it" | Overpromises our scope | "we will prepare the documents" |
| "protect you" | Legal outcome claim | "help you get the documents in place" |
| "ensure your wishes are carried out" | Legal outcome claim | "prepare documents that reflect your wishes" |
| "legal help" | Ambiguous — implies legal advice | "document preparation help," "help with your paperwork" |

### 3.3 Describing what documents do

When explaining what a document is, stick to factual descriptions of the document's function. Do not tell a client whether they need it.

**Acceptable:**
> "A Durable Power of Attorney designates someone to manage your financial affairs if you become unable to do so yourself."

**Not acceptable:**
> "You need a Durable Power of Attorney to protect yourself."

### 3.4 Disclaimer language

The footer disclaimer is legally required and should remain unchanged. It currently reads:

> *The definition excerpts provided above come directly from the Oregon State Bar web site. It is important to realize that changes may occur in this area of law. This information is not intended to be legal advice regarding your particular needs or problem, and is not intended to replace the work of an attorney. We are not attorneys and we do not offer nor do we provide legal advice. Should you require legal advice, you should contact an attorney or the Oregon State Bar Association's Lawyer Referral Service at (503) 684-3763.*

This disclaimer should appear on every page. Do not shorten it or rewrite it without legal review.

A shorter inline version is acceptable on package/pricing pages:

> "All prices are for document preparation only. We are not attorneys and do not provide legal advice."

## 4. Writing About Specific Services

### Estate Planning

Estate planning is the most emotionally sensitive area of the site. Clients are thinking about death, incapacity, and family. Language should be:

- **Matter-of-fact, not morbid.** State what things are without dramatizing.
- **Normalizing.** Estate planning is for everyone, not just the wealthy or elderly.
- **Family-focused.** Many clients are motivated by wanting to take care of people they love.

**Good framing:**
> "Estate planning is about making sure your wishes are documented clearly so the people you care about know what to do."

**Avoid:**
> "Protect your family from a legal nightmare."

#### Document descriptions (approved language)

**Last Will and Testament**
> A will directs how your property is distributed after your death and, if you have minor children, can name a guardian.

**Revocable Trust Agreement**
> A revocable trust holds your assets during your lifetime and distributes them to your named beneficiaries after your death, typically without going through probate.

**Pour-Over Will**
> A pour-over will works alongside a trust, directing any assets not already in the trust to be transferred into it at the time of death.

**Trustor's Certification and Memorandum of Trust**
> This document summarizes key provisions of your trust for third parties — such as banks or title companies — without requiring disclosure of the full trust.

**Durable General Power of Attorney**
> This document authorizes a person you name to manage your financial and legal affairs if you become incapacitated. "Durable" means it remains in effect even if you lose capacity.

**Health Care Power of Attorney**
> This authorizes a person you name to make medical decisions on your behalf if you are unable to make them yourself.

**Oregon Advance Directive**
> Oregon's standard form for documenting your end-of-life healthcare wishes, including whether you want life-sustaining treatment in specific circumstances.

### Deeds & Transfers

> Property transfer documents prepared clearly and accurately.

Keep it simple. Clients coming for deeds usually know what they need. Avoid implying legal strategy.

### Power of Attorney (standalone)

Use the approved document descriptions above. When both Durable General and Health Care POA are mentioned together, explain that they cover different areas (financial vs. medical).

## 5. Calls to Action

CTAs should be low-pressure and action-oriented. Clients are often anxious about legal paperwork. The goal is to make taking the next step feel easy and safe.

| Context | Preferred CTA |
|---|---|
| Ready to move forward | "Book an Appointment" |
| Still deciding or have questions | "Ask a Question" / "Send Us a Message" |
| Browsing services | "View Estate Packages" |
| Bottom of page | "Get Started" or "Reach Out" |

Avoid: "Get Legal Help Today," "Protect Yourself Now," or anything that creates urgency through fear.

---

## 6. Referring Clients to Attorneys

When a client's question is outside our scope, refer them clearly and without embarrassment. This is a trust-builder, not a failure.

**Referral language:**
> "That's a question an attorney can answer for you. If you don't have one, the Oregon State Bar's Lawyer Referral Service is a good starting point: (503) 684-3763."

On the website, the footer disclaimer handles this. In appointments and correspondence, use the above.
